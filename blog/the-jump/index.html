The Jump I joined the Navy because my mom couldn't afford to send me to college. More than anything, I wanted to start my adult life on a path to success. I didn't have a roadmap. I just knew I wanted a future that looked bigger than the one I could see in front of me. I wanted to prove to myself that I was capable of more. And I wanted to make my mom proud. There was just one problem. I didn't know how to swim. Not "I wasn't a strong swimmer." Not "I was a little nervous in the water." I mean I genuinely did not know how to swim. Looking back, joining the Navy without knowing how to swim seems like a pretty important detail to overlook. At eighteen, it never crossed my mind. Then I got to boot camp. One of the requirements was a swim qualification test. The instructions sounded simple enough. Jump off a ten-foot platform. Swim fifty meters. Then float face down in the water for five minutes, coming up only briefly for air while instructors splashed water in your face. One by one, people climbed the platform. Some jumped without hesitation. Others paused for a second before stepping off. Then they surfaced and made their way across the pool. I watched every single one of them. Not because I was interested. Because I was scared. Really scared. My heart was pounding. My stomach was in knots. I kept staring at that platform thinking, What am I doing here? The funny thing is, I wasn't afraid of failing the test. I wasn't worried about embarrassing myself. What scared me most was the thought of letting my mom down. The thought of calling home and telling her I couldn't do it. That I quit. That all the sacrifices she had made for me had led to me walking away the first-time things got hard. That thought hit me harder than the fear of the water ever could. So, I kept watching. I studied everyone who went before me. How they jumped. How they surfaced. How they moved through the water. I figured if I paid close enough attention, maybe I could borrow a little confidence from the people ahead of me. Eventually the line got shorter. Then it was my turn. I climbed the stairs. Walked to the edge. Looked down. For a moment, every excuse imaginable showed up. You're not ready. You can't do this. Maybe this was a mistake. Maybe you should step back. Then I thought about my mom. And I jumped. The second I hit the water; all the overthinking disappeared. There was no room left for fear. No room left for doubt. There was only one thing that mattered. Get to the other side. I wasn't confident. I wasn't calm. I wasn't even sure I knew what I was doing. I just knew quitting wasn't an option. Somehow, stroke by stroke, I made it. I've thought about that day a lot over the years. Not because of the swim test itself. Because of what it taught me. People often assume courage comes first. That you become brave and then take the leap. My experience has usually been the opposite. Most of the important moments in my life started with uncertainty. Fear. Doubt. Questions. The courage showed up after I moved. That day taught me something I'll never forget: Courage isn't the absence of fear. It's deciding that something else matters more. For me, it was making my mom proud. Even now, when I'm facing something new, something uncertain, or something that feels bigger than I am, I think about that eighteen-year-old version of me standing on the edge of a ten-foot platform. Scared. Unsure. Convinced she might fail. But willing to jump anyway. For all intents and purposes, I still can’t really swim. But I can float hella good. — CC
