Originality Is Overrated

A story about ideas, timing, collaboration, and why one person’s opinion should never be the final verdict.

For a long time, I thought original ideas were supposed to feel like lightning bolts.

Something brand new.

Something no one else had thought of.

Something so obvious once you said it out loud that everyone else would immediately see it too.

Then I took a marketing class at community college.

We were assigned a group project, and the challenge was to come up with an original idea that could help elevate a brand.

My group chose Starbucks.

At the time, the problem felt obvious to us.

The lines were long.

People were busy.

Everyone was starting to use their phones for more and more things.

So we came up with an idea.

What if Starbucks had an app?

You could download it to your phone.

You could order your drink before you got there.

You could skip the long line.

You could earn points every time you bought something.

To us, it made perfect sense.

It solved a real problem.

It created convenience.

It rewarded loyalty.

We thought we had nailed it.

Our professor did not agree.

He told us no one would ever use an app to order coffee.

Not only did he disagree with the idea, he graded our paper down because of it.

At the time, I remember thinking, “Maybe he’s right.”

Maybe the idea was too unrealistic.

Maybe we were reaching.

Maybe we missed something obvious.

Years later, Starbucks became known for exactly that kind of convenience.

Mobile ordering.

Rewards.

Points.

Skipping the line.

Ordering ahead from your phone.

Do I think Starbucks somehow caught wind of our community college project and stole the idea?

No.

That would be ridiculous.

But that experience stuck with me because it taught me something I have carried into leadership, business, and life.

Originality is overrated.

Timing, execution, belief, and persistence are what make an idea real.

At the time, I thought the idea was original.

Maybe it was.

Maybe it wasn’t.

That part matters less to me now.

What matters is that one person looked at the idea from one angle and decided it would not work.

And because that person had authority, their opinion carried weight.

That is where a lot of good ideas die.

Not because they are bad.

Not because they are impossible.

But because they are judged too quickly by too few people.

One perspective is not enough.

One opinion is not the whole truth.

One person saying “that will never work” does not mean the idea has no value.

Sometimes it means the idea needs refinement.

Sometimes it means the timing is off.

Sometimes it means you are explaining it to the wrong audience.

And sometimes it means the other person simply cannot see what you see yet.

That lesson changed how I learned to lead.

Over time, I stopped believing I needed to have every answer in the room.

I could not see every angle.

I could not catch every blind spot.

I could not understand every customer, every obstacle, or every opportunity through my perspective alone.

No leader can.

That is why collaboration matters.

Not the performative kind where leaders pretend to ask for input but have already made up their minds.

Real collaboration.

The kind where people are allowed to challenge the idea.

Improve it.

Question it.

Pressure test it.

Look at it from a completely different angle.

Because people are built differently.

They think differently.

They notice different things.

They bring different experiences, instincts, and concerns to the table.

And when you are smart enough to listen, the idea gets better.

That does not mean every idea is good.

Some ideas are bad.

Some ideas are poorly timed.

Some ideas need to be torn apart and rebuilt before they are worth anything.

But dismissing an idea too quickly is lazy leadership.

The better question is not, “Will this work?”

The better question is, “What would have to be true for this to work?”

That question opens the door.

It invites curiosity.

It forces people to think beyond what already exists.

And sometimes, that is where the best ideas live.

Not in being original.

Not in being right immediately.

But in being willing to look again.

From another angle.

With another perspective.

With enough belief to keep going, and enough humility to make the idea better.

Because the future almost always sounds unrealistic before it becomes normal.

— CC
